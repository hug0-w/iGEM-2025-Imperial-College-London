Engineering

Engineering Cycle

Cycle 1

Design: Primers were designed to span across both the backbone and the insert.

Build: Golden Gate assembly of the plasmid constructs was performed using a cycling protocol.

Test: Ran colony PCR and visualised the results using gel electrophoresis. The gel returned empty.

Learn: As successful insertion of the plasmid backbone was required for the colony PCR to produce any band, we were unable to troubleshoot this result very effectively. Specifically, we were unable to conclude whether the negative gel was due to a problem in the colony PCR protocol or a problem with assembly and transformation.

Cycle 2

Design: We ordered new primers that bind only to the backbone, where transformation of an empty or correct backbone plasmid would show a smaller band in the gel. As the primers bind the region which flanks the gene insert, successful transformants can be identified through a larger sized amplicon.

Build: We re-ran the Golden Gate assembly of plasmid using the same cycling protocol.

Test: We transformed E. coli and performed colony PCR with the new primers on the transformants to assess correct assembly.

Learn: We were able to identify whether the issue leading to our low number of successful transformants was an assembly or a transformation issue. We found that the CRISPR plasmids had assembled correctly but there was an issue in assembly with the growth factor plasmids.

Cycle 3

Design: We researched new protocols for Golden Gate assemblies with higher efficiencies when using type 2B restriction enzymes. We swapped out to only use NEB restriction enzymes to ensure the buffer used is appropriate for efficient activity of both enzymes.

Build: We re-ran the Golden Gate assembly of plasmid and performed it using the same cycling protocol.

Test: We transformed E. coli and performed colony PCR on the transformants to assess correct assembly.

Learn: There was increased transformation efficiency and we observed fewer colonies on the control (empty) assembly transformation, indicating the plasmid backbone may have been enzymatically linearised during assembly. However, when we ran colony PCR as described in the previous DBTL cycle, the gel had distinct bands, but all the plasmids were negative for the correct assembly. We concluded that there must be some problem with the assembly, and after checking our cloning plans, realised that we were not accounting for the length of the plasmid backbone the parts were shipped in during our Golden Gate assembly calculations, which affected the fragment volumes required for high efficiency assembly.

Cycle 4

Design: We re-calculated the Golden Gate assembly protocol to account for the plasmid backbone length.

Build: We re-ran Golden Gate assembly with the updated reagent volumes.

Test: We transformed E. coli and performed colony PCR on the transformants to assess correct assembly.

Learn: We saw bands which indicated distinct larger amplicons on some of the colonies, indicating that some assemblies had worked. Namely, EGF, HGF, and IGF-Rho all showed successful assembly. We theorised that we had run out of usable FGF plasmid by this point, and that the concentration of mCherry plasmid was lower than what we measured by Nanodrop quantification. We chose to inoculate an overnight culture of TOP10 with mCherry plasmid to amplify and obtain a higher concentration.

Hoc

Cycle 1

Design: Articles indicated a truncated Hoc1 led to a more permeable cell membrane. We investigated the biochemical mechanism for this difference.

Build: AlphaFold3 was used to generate structure predictions for both truncated and native Hoc1 in K. phaffii. Generated structure exhibited high confidence level as the structure of Hoc1 homologs in multiple species have been resolved.

Test: We visualised the Hoc1 protein using PyMol to predict the position of the active site. Aligning both structures showed truncation affected the shape of the active site, which would affect enzymatic activity.

Learn: This result validates our initial thought that a truncated Hoc1 has different levels of enzymatic activity compared to native Hoc1.

Cycle 2

Design: Designed corresponding sgRNAs targeting the hoc1 locus, using homology arms which flank the upstream and downstream elements of the gene. Gene fragments were designed to have high-fidelity Golden Gate overhangs for assembly.

Build: DNA fragments were assembled using overlap extension PCR.

Test: Construct sequence length was verified by gel electrophoresis.

Learn: Plasmid construction was successful. We moved on to Golden Gate assembly.

Cycle 3

Design: Golden Gate assembly was designed to construct the final sgRNA–Cas9 plasmid.

Build: Golden Gate assembly with BbsI enzyme was performed, after which the plasmids were transformed into chemically competent TOP10 E. coli.

Test: Observed colonies on corresponding antibiotic selection plates and sequence verified assemblies.

Learn: E. coli transformation was successful, and we were able to move on to electrocompetent K. phaffii transformation.

Cycle 4

Design: We designed a protocol and prepared electrocompetent K. phaffii for electroporation.

Build: We transformed K. phaffii with sgRNA plasmid and HR template.

Test: Plated transformants onto Hygromycin and Hygromycin + Nourseothricin (NRS) combination plates to select for transformants containing the Cas9 plasmid. Colonies which survived were then plated onto NRS plates to select for the gene insert.

Learn: Colonies grew on Hygromycin plates but were unable to proliferate on Nourseothricin plates. This indicates that while the Cas9–sgRNA plasmids were correctly transformed, we had insufficient concentrations of the HR templates causing the integration of the cassette to fail.

Cycle 5

Design: We adjusted the protocol to increase the concentration of DNA present during K. phaffii electroporation.

Build: We ran K. phaffii electroporation.

Test: Plated transformants onto Hygromycin and Hygromycin + Nourseothricin (NRS) combination plates to select for transformants containing the Cas9 plasmid, where there were no colonies that survived.

Learn: As we used a different electroporator model and conducted the protocol without direct supervision, we considered possible issues deriving from the machinery. Upon reviewing the settings of the electroporator, we identified that the time constant varied from our initial run, which likely decreased K. phaffii cell viability.

Cycle 6

Design: We continued to use the higher plasmid DNA concentration and conducted the electroporation protocol under the correct settings.

Build: We ran K. phaffii electroporation.

Test: Observed colonies which were able to grow on the combination Hygromycin and Nourseothricin selection plates, indicating potentially successful transformants.

Learn: The modifications we planned (Hoc1 repair + downregulation, PMI downregulation) may be viable in K. phaffii. Further testing is required to confirm these modifications have taken place.
