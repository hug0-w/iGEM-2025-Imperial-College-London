DRY LAB

No-Code Bayesian Optimisation Workflow

During our experiments we recognised that having a readily accessible optimisation model would streamline decisions on media composition, incubation times, and the upkeep of both bioreactors and shake flasks. A review of scientific literature and past iGEM contributions suggested that a generalised, no-code batch Bayesian optimisation workflow would offer a distinctive capability to the community. This led us to develop BioKernel, our no-code interface for experiment optimisation (explore the model).

To validate BioKernel, we designed an experiment focused on optimising inducer concentrations within a metabolic pathway controlled by orthogonal inputs. The foundation for this experiment was the “Marionette” strain of E. coli, which offers twelve highly orthogonal and engineered small-molecule sensors for precise pathway control.[1] Using this chassis, we targeted the production of astaxanthin, a carotenoid measurable in 96-well plate assays without cell lysis, enabling high-throughput characterisation of our optimisation routine.[2]

Astaxanthin as a Final Target for Quantification

The carotenoid pathway is well-characterised and generates pigments whose concentrations can be determined colorimetrically. Previous work achieved high beta-carotene titres in E. coli by co-expressing the mevalonate (MVA) and methylerythritol 4-phosphate (MEP) pathways,[3] and subsequent optimisation of the MEP pathway alone enabled industrially relevant isoprene production.[4] We initially explored recreating this dual-pathway approach, but the additional enzyme burden would exceed the Marionette strain’s capacity of twelve orthogonal regulators.

We therefore restricted our experiment to the endogenous MEP pathway, relying on Bayesian optimisation to navigate complex parameter interactions while maintaining a manageable circuit size. Literature benchmarking also indicated that astaxanthin diffuses across the plasma membrane more readily than other carotenoids, and its extracellular concentration strongly correlates with intracellular levels (R² = 0.9548).[2] By introducing the enzymes CrtW and CrtZ, we redirected the beta-carotene pathway towards astaxanthin, securing a quantifiable readout that suits rapid iteration with BioKernel.

Further Optimisation of Experimental Plan

Ensuring that measured outputs reflected only changes in inducer concentrations required additional genetic design considerations. We added insulators to each transcriptional unit to maintain orthogonality and to shield our circuits from unintended enhancer or silencer effects originating from neighbouring sequences. We selected eleven orthogonal insulators described by Nielsen et al. to support this architecture.[5]

To further tighten control, we standardised translational initiation rates across the pathway. We designed batches of ribosome binding sites using the De Novo DNA RBS Calculator, targeting a translation initiation rate of 50,000 for each coding sequence while providing the calculator with the upstream promoter-insulator context and the full enzyme CDS.[6] Together, these design choices give us confidence that BioKernel’s optimisation cycles would map cleanly onto biological responses once wet-lab execution resumes.
