WET LAB

Experimental Design

We conducted three key experiments for our overall proof of concept:

  • The CRISPR/Cas9-based engineering of EPS-related pathways to minimise exopolysaccharide production and secretion.
  • The production of recombinant growth factors from K. phaffii for use in the cultivated meat industry.
  • Optimisation of an enzymatic pathway in E. coli as validation for a no-code Bayesian optimisation workflow for future K. phaffii metabolic engineering and media optimisation.

These experimental lines together span our integrated pipeline — from strain-level genetic engineering and recombinant protein production to automation and optimisation of bioprocesses.

Strain Engineering K.phaffii

Show Details

We spread our engineering strategy such that it addressed the most mechanistically plausible causes for EPS secretion, which to date have not been conclusively identified in literature.

  • Our first theory was that the truncated Hoc1 enzyme results in less glycan branching and consequently more free mannans in the extracellular space.
  • Secondly, a cell wall stress response due to thinner cell walls may drive excess mannans into the Golgi apparatus.
  • Finally, if neither mechanism proved true, we aimed to reduce intracellular mannose synthesis directly by downregulating its metabolic precursor pathways.

Why We Target Exopolysaccharides

Through both our integrated human practices with Magnify Bio and Liberation Bioindustries and a review of recent literature, we identified exopolysaccharides (EPS) as a critical impurity complicating downstream processing in K. phaffii-based production systems.

  • EPS impurities co-migrate with recombinant proteins in size-based purification such as micro- or ultrafiltration, greatly complicating scalable separation[10–12].
  • They can agglomerate with proteins and form dense “cake” layers on membranes, leading to fouling and yield loss[12].
  • Even high-effort recovery methods like anion exchange fail when charge distributions overlap[12].
  • These impurities are increasingly recognised as major cost drivers in scaling recombinant protein production[10].
  • Up to 4% of cell dry weight may consist of these unwanted mannans[10,12], representing both a yield and energy loss.

To our knowledge, no previous studies have addressed EPS secretion at the strain-engineering level — representing a significant opportunity to improve downstream efficiency for multiple protein products.

Accordingly, we decided on one genetic target per mechanistic hypothesis, resulting in three distinct modification strategies:

  • Hoc1 — a 1,6-mannosyltransferase responsible for branching of glycan arms on glycoproteins.
  • PMI — phosphomannose isomerase, linking fructose-6-phosphate to mannose-6-phosphate (GDP-mannose synthesis).
  • Rho1 — a GTPase in the cell wall integrity pathway, modulating wall biosynthesis and stress response.

Because the Hoc1 truncation in industrial strains improves secretion and transformability, we avoided restoring full-length activity. Instead, we aimed to downregulate Hoc1 to reduce EPS while retaining secretion performance.

We acknowledged potential trade-offs — such as tighter cell walls reducing secretion efficiency or altered central metabolism slowing growth — and therefore distributed our engineering targets across metabolic, structural, and regulatory layers for balanced impact.

Engineering Approach

To modify Hoc1 and PMI, we adopted the CRISPR/Cas9 system as a modular and well-characterised editing tool. However, given commercial patent constraints around CRISPR, we evaluated split-marker homologous recombination as a viable, license-free alternative that achieves the same edits through native yeast recombination mechanisms.

Following Gassler et al.[1], we designed ten sgRNA devices with flanking 5′ hammerhead (HH) and 3′ hepatitis delta virus (HDV) ribozymes for efficient self-excision and processing. Target regions were selected around the start and stop codons of Hoc1 and PMI to minimise unintended modifications.

For Rho1, we planned a mild overexpression under the weak constitutive promoter pPGK1 to partially reinforce cell wall integrity without impairing cell proliferation[2].

By combining these strategies, our engineered strains aim to reduce EPS secretion at the source, improve recombinant protein yield, and maintain commercial feasibility by avoiding patent-encumbered technologies.

Production of Recombinant Growth Factors

Show Details

Our growth factor experiment planned to demonstrate the ability of K. phaffii to produce recombinant mammalian growth factors at high titres and to characterise the production of HGF and IGF-1 in K. phaffii for the first time in iGEM.

We chose to target four growth factors based on their roles in muscle growth and tissue regeneration:

  • Epidermal Growth Factor (EGF) — stimulates cell proliferation and differentiation[3].
  • Basic Fibroblast Growth Factor (bFGF) — promotes strong proliferation and migration of myocytes[4].
  • Hepatocyte Growth Factor (HGF) — activates muscle satellite cells and supports regeneration[5].
  • Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1) — promotes cell proliferation in muscle and bone[6].

Secretion Strategy

We engineered these growth factors to be secreted using a pre-Ost1–pro-α mating factor fusion secretion tag. Traditionally, K. phaffii relies on the full α-mating factor from S. cerevisiae to mediate protein secretion, but this sequence can slow translocation of large proteins such as FGF2 or HGF and may cause aggregation during high-volume expression.

Based on Barrero et al. (2018), we employed a hybrid signal consisting of the pre-factor of Ost1 fused to the pro-region of the α-mating factor, resulting in up to 20× higher secretion efficiency for some proteins[7].

Purification and Tagging

To facilitate purification, we appended a C-terminal poly-histidine (6×His) tag using a flexible G₄S×₃ linker. This design enables nickel spin column affinity chromatography for efficient recovery of growth factors.

While His-tags are sometimes immunogenic, our rationale for using them was twofold:

  • We do not intend therapeutic use, so minor immunogenicity poses no risk.
  • His-tag purification is a low-cost, low-labour method ideal for lab-scale validation.

Together, this secretion and purification design ensured our constructs would combine high yield, process scalability, and experimental practicality.

Strain Map

The proposed experimental pathway resulted in several distinct K. phaffii strains for characterisation:

  • CRISPR transformants — strains with downregulated or repaired Hoc1 and PMI genes.
  • Rho1-engineered constructs — co-expressing Rho1 or mCherry (control) alongside growth factor plasmids.
  • Growth factor expression strains — producing EGF, bFGF, HGF, or IGF-1 under secretion and His-tag constructs.

We prioritised bFGF for initial validation due to its previously characterised size and secretion profile, with HGF as a secondary focus if secretion proved feasible without additional chaperone expression.

To verify biological activity, we planned to assess the proliferative potential of our recombinant growth factors in bovine muscle cells, comparing their effects against commercial FBS and recombinant standards (e.g., from E. coli or CHO systems). Unfortunately, this was not completed due to time limitations.

Downstream Characterisation

Show Details

Following the production of recombinant growth factors in K. phaffii, we designed a downstream workflow to quantify and compare the production of both recombinant proteins (desired products) and exopolysaccharides (side products) across our engineered strains.

Our goal was to correlate EPS reduction with improved protein purity and yield — providing measurable confirmation that our strain-level engineering translated to bioprocessing advantages.

EPS Quantification

We developed a colorimetric dual-assay workflow to estimate the concentration of extracellular polysaccharides, most of which are mannans[11]. Our approach involved quantifying both total sugars and reducing sugars, where the difference between the two represents the polysaccharide fraction.

  • Phenol–Sulfuric Acid (PSA) Assay[13] — quantifies total carbohydrate concentration by acid hydrolysis of all saccharides.
  • Dinitrosalicylic Acid (DNS) Assay[14] — quantifies reducing sugars through a redox reaction with 3,5-dinitrosalicylic acid.

By running these assays in parallel, we could determine the approximate proportion of EPS within each supernatant sample, allowing comparison between engineered and control strains under identical conditions.

Protein Purification and Quantification

To isolate recombinant growth factors, we employed His-tag Ni–NTA affinity chromatography, leveraging the C-terminal 6×His tags fused to each construct via the G₄S(x3) flexible linker domain.

We planned to use HisPur Ni–NTA resin (Thermo) to capture His-tagged proteins directly from the supernatant, followed by elution and quantification. Protein concentration was to be measured using the Bradford assay, enabling total growth factor quantification across samples.

To ensure assay reliability, we planned to include controls using unmodified K. phaffii (PN-2 strain) cultures — both purified and unpurified — to identify background interference from native His-rich proteins.

For advanced compositional analysis, we also prepared to send samples for HPLC characterisation to confirm the molecular weight and identity of produced proteins and polysaccharides. However, due to time constraints, this analysis was not completed during the project timeline.

Despite limited experimental execution, this pipeline provides a complete validation framework for future teams: one that couples strain-level EPS mitigation with protein yield enhancement through direct quantification of both intended and unintended outputs in recombinant protein production.
