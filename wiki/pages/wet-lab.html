WET LAB

Experimental Design

We conducted three key experimental lines of work to validate our overall proof of concept:

  • CRISPR/Cas9-based engineering of EPS-related pathways to minimise exopolysaccharide production and secretion.
  • Production of recombinant growth factors in K. phaffii for cultivated meat bioprocessing.
  • Validation of a no-code Bayesian optimisation workflow using E. coli for pathway tuning and media optimisation.

These experiments represent three scales of our integrated pipeline — from strain engineering and recombinant protein production to automation and optimisation.

Strain Engineering of K. phaffii

Show Details

We designed our engineering strategy to target the most mechanistically plausible causes of exopolysaccharide (EPS) secretion in K. phaffii, as the exact mechanism remains uncharacterised in literature.

Our hypotheses included: (1) the truncated Hoc1 enzyme causing under-branched glycans and excess free mannans; (2) a cell wall stress response upregulating mannose secretion; and (3) excessive intracellular mannose production driving accumulation of secreted polysaccharides.

To test these, we selected one protein target for each scenario:

  • Hoc1 — 1,6-mannosyltransferase involved in glycan branching.
  • PMI — phosphomannose isomerase, linking glycolysis and GDP-mannose synthesis.
  • Rho1 — GTPase in the cell wall integrity pathway, influencing cell rigidity.

Our aim was not to restore full cell wall rigidity, but to mitigate EPS impurity formation while maintaining the secretion efficiency that makes K. phaffii a valuable host.

To implement these modifications, we used the CRISPR/Cas9 system due to its reliability and modularity. However, for scalability, we also evaluated split-marker integration as an open, license-free alternative.

Following Gassler et al. (2019)[1], we designed 10 sgRNA devices flanked by 5′ hammerhead and 3′ hepatitis delta virus ribozymes for efficient self-excision. Targets were positioned near start and stop codons to minimise unintended off-target effects.

For Rho1, we planned a mild overexpression using the weak constitutive promoter pPGK1, intended to partially restore cell wall integrity without compromising cell growth[2].

Figure 1. Overview of genetic engineering strategy targeting Hoc1, PMI, and Rho1.

This experimental framework ensured our edits spanned metabolic, structural, and regulatory pathways, maximising the chance of identifying and mitigating the root causes of EPS secretion.

Production of Recombinant Growth Factors in K. phaffii

Show Details

Our growth factor experiment aimed to demonstrate the ability of K. phaffii to express and secrete mammalian growth factors at high titres, and to characterise HGF and IGF-1 expression for the first time in iGEM.

We selected four growth factors due to their distinct biological properties and relevance to cultivated meat:

  • Epidermal Growth Factor (EGF) — induces mammalian cell proliferation[3].
  • Basic Fibroblast Growth Factor (bFGF) — promotes strong proliferation and migration[4].
  • Hepatocyte Growth Factor (HGF) — activates muscle satellite cells and promotes regeneration[5].
  • Insulin-like Growth Factor-1 (IGF-1) — induces cell proliferation in muscle and bone[6].

Each factor was designed for secretion using the pre-Ost1–pro-α-mating factor hybrid signal, which enhances ER translocation efficiency and reduces aggregation during secretion.

The pre-Ost1 signal sequence from S. cerevisiae was chosen based on the work of Barrero et al. (2018), who demonstrated up to 20-fold higher secretion compared to the traditional α-mating factor[7].

Figure 2. Construct design for secretion of recombinant growth factors using the pre-Ost1–pro-α signal.

To enable purification, a C-terminal 6×His-tag was fused to each construct via a flexible G₄S×₃ linker. This facilitates Ni-NTA affinity chromatography for downstream purification.

Although His-tags can exhibit immunogenic effects, we justified their use since:

  • Our products are intended for industrial, not therapeutic use.
  • The His-tag offers a cost-effective and reliable purification method requiring minimal labour.

The final constructs integrate secretion efficiency, structural stability, and downstream compatibility — positioning K. phaffii as a feasible host for growth factor production in animal-free media.

Validating a No-Code Bayesian Optimisation Workflow

Show Details

To demonstrate our computational design–wet lab integration, we designed a validation experiment to test the BioKernel Bayesian optimisation (BO) workflow using E. coli as a model system. This approach would allow us to assess how BO can efficiently tune enzyme expression levels and inducer concentrations in complex metabolic pathways — paving the way for similar optimisation in K. phaffii.

The experiment was designed to be both in silico and in vitro, combining machine learning–driven prediction with experimental readouts to iteratively improve enzyme ratios for astaxanthin biosynthesis.

Experimental Setup

We utilised the Marionette-Wild MG1655 strain of E. coli[8], which contains twelve orthogonal, small-molecule–inducible sensors to fine-tune transcriptional activity. This system allows precise control over enzyme expression using independent chemical inducers — ideal for an optimisation framework.

For plasmid construction, we used the EcoFlex Modular Cloning (MoClo) kit[9] developed by the Freemont Lab at Imperial College London. This system provides a Golden Gate–based assembly workflow with RFP negative selection to confirm successful ligation.

We planned the assembly in three stages:

  • Stage 1: 10 transcriptional units, each encoding an enzyme under a unique promoter.
  • Stage 2: Two intermediate constructs, each containing five assembled Stage 1 parts.
  • Stage 3: A final plasmid combining both Stage 2 assemblies into a single expression vector.
Figure 3. EcoFlex MoClo assembly workflow used for BioKernel validation experiments.

The assembly’s success can be visually verified using colorimetric RFP negative selection — colonies that lose the red phenotype indicate successful Golden Gate assembly.

Astaxanthin Production Assay

The astaxanthin pathway was selected as the model biosynthetic circuit, since its product can be quantified colorimetrically at 490 nm without cell lysis — allowing rapid, high-throughput screening in microplates.

Figure 4. Astaxanthin biosynthetic pathway selected for Bayesian optimisation validation.

The experimental workflow involved growing the transformed E. coli cultures in 96-well plates overnight, followed by direct measurement of pigment intensity via absorbance. This setup provides the BO algorithm with measurable fitness feedback.

Although time constraints prevented the physical execution of this experiment, our design establishes a foundation for data-driven bioprocess optimisation and demonstrates the feasibility of integrating BioKernel with synthetic biology workflows.

Future iGEM teams can extend this framework to optimise enzyme balance, growth media, and induction strategies for any recombinant system — bridging computational design and experimental realisation.
